## Algorithm

The current straight-line solution mainly combines **Hough transform** and **Bezier curve fitting**. Hough transform is the main method, while Bezier fitting plays a supporting role.

### Hough transform

Hough transform is mainly used to extract the track centerline. It accumulates votes from the perception output and selects the most credible straight-line structure.

**Core idea**: the two strongest straight lines are treated as the left and right track boundaries, which then define the route we want.

[Hough transform tutorial video](https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV1k44y1G772/)

### Bezier fitting

Bezier fitting helps generate a smoother and straighter target path. When the detected road segment is not perfectly straight, it can still fit a better straight-line approximation quickly.

[Bezier curves as an interpolation method](https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV1YQCvYTEmT/)

## Future optimization direction

Besides spending more time on real tuning, one promising improvement path is the following.

### State-prediction-based filtering

Introduce a state-estimation method such as a Kalman filter.

[Kalman filter: from theory to practice](https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV1Rh41117MT/)

**Idea**
- stop treating the Hough-fit track line as the only decision source
- use inertial-navigation motion information for internal state prediction
- fuse the two sources into a more reliable track estimate
- if the goal is simply to finish the run, the car can drive the full track more robustly even at a lower speed

## Review focus

<Aside type="tip">
Compared with the previous year, judges now appear to care slightly less about algorithm detail alone and more about how the team validated and tuned the system.
</Aside>

### What matters more now

- your **redundancy measures**
- your **tuning workflow**
- whether you kept **detailed records**
- your **issue-resolution process**
- whether you can provide **real evidence**

<Aside type="note">
Judges increasingly want concrete, traceable engineering evidence rather than abstract claims.
</Aside>
